    Today we're going to cover the broad strokes of how user story maps work and the bigger ideas that can help you transform the way your team works.

    We're going to use a simple example to learn about building the story of your application. And I will show you a simple map that illustrates a project I'm working on.

    What is a Story Map?

    A diagram of a project that tells the story of the people and systems involved in a process.

    Detail is added as we learn more about the project

    The map can be built for an existing application or a new application.
  • A visual representation of the narrative flow of the application and processes that happen inside and outside.
  • (*) We can discover many details about the project
  • It highlights areas where you need to know more, and allows you to investigate that before you begin writing any code.
  • (*)This technique works for new and existing projects of any size.

    • The Story Map
  • Structurally, your map will look a bit like this.
  • The story flows from left to right
  • Details and steps are built from the top down.
  • You can show users of the application, participants in the process, or even a background system process
  • When we get the full story described, we will begin to slice it. But we'll talk about that soon.

    • Who should Story Map?

    Anyone who knows the process ... Not Just Developers

    At least one knowledgeable person from each group of stakeholders
  • Our apps often automated processes.
  • You want to bring to your story mapping discussion anyone who knows the processes you're automating.
  • (*)The room should actually have fewer programmers in the beginning unless they can be mostly silent.
  • Developers shouldn't fill in the story, but ask questions.
  • You cannot learn what your application is doing or supposed to be doing from people who don't use it to solve a problem they have.
  • This doesn't mean to keep them out of the loop of discovery, but their role is quieter here

    • When to Story Map?

     

    • When you have questions
    • Before You begin development
  • Any time you are doing work and don't know the business process behind it.
  • The application can already exist, and you can use this to discover how new features or fixes should be implemented.

    • Why Story Map?
    • Build shared understanding
    • Encourage full discovery before prototyping
    • Prioritize work to be done as a group

    *Lowers problems with estimates and feature creep
  • Story mapping lets you discover the full project
  • It highlights question areas, so you can answer them before development begins
  • enables the whole group to prioritize work and removes technical details from that discussion
  • (*)Discovery up front is less expensive than discovery in the middle of coding

    Learn More
    • Risk
    • Assumptions
    • Uncertainty

    If you cannot elaborate, mark it and revisit
  • Once you've fleshed out the summary of what you're describing, you need to fill in the detail
  • There are a lot of ways to add more detail, and figure out what you're missing
  • You'll look for areas of Risk. It doesn't have to stop planning or development, but it will help you to plan and prepare
  • are there parts of your map that you're making assumptions about your application?
  • Are there things you don't know?
  • I usually mark assumptions and uncertainty with ? and Risk with a red post-it with an ! on it

    • Clarifying Questions
    • What could we learn to replace risk with REAL information?
    • Do we really know what has been mapped, or did we fill in assumptions?
    • Are you sure about the story you're telling?
  • To clear these marks off your map, you can ask yourself, your customers/users, etc questions
  • You want to replace the risk with real information; and where you cannot remove the risk, you plan to do less work at the time you take on that risk
  • Are the stories and tasks in your map based on REAL information? Or were there assumptions being made?
  • How certain are you about the story you're telling?

    • Thorough Discovery
    • Understand the full process
      • Understand "why" steps are needed in the process
      • Talk about things inside and outside of the app
  • This whole process is investigation, and it should include your customers and users. Remember this isn't implementation

    • Thorough Discovery
    • Simplify and lower risk at implementation
      • Lowers the questions at the phase of implementation
      • Limits Feature Creep (beginning implementation w/o understanding, new features come in)
  • That understanding and investigation reduces the number of questions when you're implementing the code
  • It should limit feature creep because when you're writing that code, you can focus only on the section in the current slice.

    • Thorough Discovery
    • Better estimates
      • Discovery is cheaper at the beginning of a project, than during implementation
  • The more complete your investigation in the beginning, the more time you save at implementation.
  • And all of this saves you money, too

    Anatomy of a Story Map

    • Users => actors in the story
    • Backbone => user activities
    • Walking Skeleton(blue) => major user tasks
    • User Stories => add detail to the story
    • Releases => organize and prioritize stories
    • Priorities => Lower Priority stories sink to the bottom

    Priorities
    • Loosely prioritize stories based on everyone's needs
    • Lower priorities sink
  • But we have seen that this gets swayed heavily by the person who wants the feature. Politics are heavy at a university.
    • S

    Prioritizing Features
    • Differentiator - feature sets you apart from competition
    • Spoiler - moves in on someone else’s differentiator
    • Cost reducer - reduces organizational costs
    • Table stakes - feature necessary to compete
  • You're going to be discussing these priorities with customers, users and developers. And sometimes they have competing interests
  • Jeff Patton recommends a labeling strategy that is really great at specifying what type of priority it is
  • Some features will help you stand above the competition; those are differentiators
  • Some will level the playing field if a competitor has a feature you didn't already have
  • Some features with save time or money and are therefore cost reducers
  • Finally, table stakes are features that you ABSOLUTELY must have in order to compete in your market

    • Prioritizing the Project
    • Who will use this product?
    • What steps must they accomplish for success?
    • Remove/postpone the rest
  • Sometimes these discussions of priorities can become personal. And it is helpful to refocus sometimes
  • In addition to showing how the features set you apart, remember who is using the product!
  • What will make you the most successful, get subscriptions, help you corner a target market?
  • Any features that aren't delivering recognizable value should be removed or postponed indefinitely
  • note: This doesn't mean with a legacy project you deprioritize refactoring and code improvements that make you're product stable or reliable

    • Focus on Outcomes
    • What are you hoping to do with your application?
    • Prioritize features based on the problem they solve
    • Implement only what solves the problem or meets the objective
  • It is so much easier to discuss priorities from a goal-oriented perspective of problem-solving
  • You have the opportunity to focus the features specifically on that goal, and it weeds out a lot of the nice-to-have tasks that slip in from the side

    • Prototyping
  • This strategy helped influence the way I plan projects and also how I prototype

    • Prototyping
    • What is the smallest thing you could build to prove/disprove an assumption?
    • Sketch & prototype to test viability of the solution
    • Aim for less than minimum, get feedback, and iterate often
    • When you give prototype to development partners you can include metrics to see what they actually do
  • When you begin developing for the project, a prototype is a lightweight way to prove you are moving in the right direction. You may plan to implement a feature with a little extra time for exploration.

    • Strategies

    opening game strategy

    • When the number of features is too large, you can cut a slice across that gives you the minimum end-to-end functionality;
    • It doesn’t solve all user’s stories, but it affects the largest number of stories.
    • With this product, they can have test data, begin testing it for load, and see how it will work

    midgame strategy

    • fill in and round out features
    • support optional steps
    • implement tough business rules
    • continue testing the product usability

    endgame strategy

    • refine: make it look more polished and efficient
    • it’s here that you’ll apply the feedback from users.

  • you will focus on solving problems and delivering real value!
